Species Overview & Home Range

üî¥ Key Characteristics

  • **Size:** Large freshwater turtle, carapace (top shell) up to 13-15 inches.
  • **Appearance:** Named for its distinct red, orange, or pale yellow **plastron** (bottom shell). Carapace is typically dark olive or brown.
  • **Distinction:** Has a prominent **notch** at the tip of the upper jaw, bordered by a tooth-like cusp.
  • **Status:** Federally **Endangered** (since 1987) and the **Official State Reptile of Alabama** (since 1990).
  • **Diet:** Primarily **herbivorous**, feeding on submerged aquatic vegetation (like hydrilla and eel-grass).

üåä Habitat & Location

  • **Home:** Critically restricted to the **Mobile-Tensaw River Delta** ecosystem.
  • **In Alabama:** Found almost exclusively in **Mobile** and **Baldwin** Counties.
  • **Preference:** Shallow, slow-moving, **freshwater** (to slightly brackish) backwaters, rivers, and bayous.
  • **Crucial Features:** Requires abundant submerged aquatic vegetation (for food) and sun-exposed **basking logs** (for thermoregulation).

Nesting Habits & Aquatic Movement

ü•ö Nesting Details

  • **Season:** Typically from **April to early August**, peaking in June and July.
  • **Location:** Females leave the water to nest on dry, sandy land: natural levees, sandbars, and especially **dredge spoil sites** (like Gravine Island).
  • **Nest:** A shallow nest is excavated, usually containing **4 to 15 eggs** (average clutch size ~13).
  • **Hatching:** Hatchlings emerge in the fall (September-November), but those laid late in the season often **overwinter** in the nest until the following spring.
  • **Major Threat:** Nest predation is extremely high (>90%) by raccoons, feral hogs, fish crows, and fire ants.

üí® Movement and Behavior

  • **Primary Motion:** Excellent swimmers, using their webbed feet as powerful paddles in the water.
  • **Land Motion:** Slow and deliberate on land. They pull themselves using their legs, leaving a characteristic trail, especially in soft sand.
  • **Basking:** They spend significant time sunning themselves on logs or floating vegetation to regulate body temperature. They are **highly wary** while basking and will quickly submerge at the slightest disturbance.
üí° **AI Note:** Their movement pattern on land (from water to nest and back) is predictable, making the tracks they leave along banks and spoil sites crucial for detection.

Major Threats & Recovery Efforts

üî• Primary Threats

  • **Habitat Loss:** Dredging, pollution, and development in the Mobile-Tensaw Delta reducing vegetated backwaters and basking sites.
  • **Vehicle Mortality:** Females nesting along the Mobile Bay Causeway (US 90/98) are frequently killed crossing the road.
  • **Nest Predation:** Extremely high rates of predation on eggs by feral hogs, raccoons, and fire ants due to concentrated nesting sites.
  • **Hybridization:** Mixing with other *Pseudemys* species (like river cooters) due to habitat changes, reducing the genetic uniqueness of the species.
  • **Sea Level Rise:** Increasing salinity in brackish areas due to climate change may make habitat less suitable.

üõ°Ô∏è Conservation Actions

  • **Protective Fencing:** Installation of chain-link fences along the Mobile Causeway to funnel turtles to safe crossing areas, significantly reducing mortality.
  • **Habitat Management:** Protecting and restoring key basking, feeding, and nesting sites, particularly around Gravine Island.
  • **Population Monitoring:** Systematic sampling and tracking (where your AI model comes in!) to assess population trend and health.
  • **Predator Control:** Management programs to control feral hog populations on key nesting islands.
