1. The Flattened Musk Turtle (Sternotherus depressus)

The Flattened Musk Turtle is one of Alabama's most unique and imperiled reptiles. It is endemic, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world.

Conservation Status

  • **Federally Listed:** Threatened (ESA, 1987)
  • **IUCN Status:** Critically Endangered
  • **Threat:** Habitat degradation (siltation from mining/agriculture) is the biggest threat.

2. Physical Description & Unique Adaptation

Description

  • **Size:** Small aquatic turtle, carapace (upper shell) usually $7.5 - 10$ cm ($3 - 4$ in) long.
  • **Color:** Carapace is dark brown to orange with dark-bordered seams. Plastron (lower shell) is pink to yellowish.
  • **Musk Glands:** Like its relatives, it can release a smelly excretion when harassed (hence "musk turtle").

Primary Diet

They are omnivorous, but rely heavily on **aquatic mollusks** (snails and mussels). The decline of these food sources due to pollution severely impacts the turtle's survival.

The Flattened Carapace

The species name *depressus* refers to its uniquely **low and flat shell**. This adaptation is a survival strategy, allowing the turtle to easily slip into narrow rock crevices and under submerged slabs to hide from predators and avoid being washed away by strong currents.

3. Habitat and Range in Alabama

Geographic Range (Endemic to Black Warrior River System)

The turtle is restricted to the **upper Black Warrior River Basin** in north-central and west-central Alabama (above Bankhead Dam).

Key Areas Include:

  • **Sipsey Fork** (populations protected by Bankhead National Forest)
  • **Locust Fork** and **Mulberry Fork** (though populations here have seen serious declines)
  • Margins of **Smith Lake** (especially areas with shelf-like rocks)

Ideal Habitat Requirements

  • **Water:** Clear, clean, free-flowing medium-sized creeks or small rivers.
  • **Depth:** Vegetated shallows about $0.6$ m ($2$ ft) deep, alternating with pools $1.1 - 1.5$ m ($3.6 - 5$ ft) deep.
  • **Substrate:** Abundant bedrock, submerged rocks, boulders, and cobble substrate. The rock crevices provide essential cover.

4. Movement and Activity Patterns

Locomotion & Home Range

  • **Movement:** They have webbed feet and can swim, crawl along the bottom, and occasionally climb logs or bask on rocks.
  • **Average Home Range:** Approximately $332$ meters (stream length) based on radio telemetry studies.
  • **Core Use Area:** Tends to be much smaller, often less than $100$ meters.

Activity Triggers

  • **Daily Cycle:** Adults are mostly **nocturnal** (active late evening and night) to avoid heat and predators like otters. Juveniles are more diurnal.
  • **Weather:** Movement distance increases with **light or moderate precipitation** and higher water temperatures.
  • **Season:** Movement and activity are greatest during the **breeding and nesting season** (April-July).

Footprint & Tracking Model Data

Since this species is primarily aquatic and uses rocky substrate, finding clear footprints (or tracks in sand/mud) is rare. Your tracking model should focus on **radio telemetry data** and **habitat use**:

  • Model movement based on **distance/stream length** ($332$m home range).
  • Model **crevice use**—they favor narrow rock refuges ($100\%$ availability is critical).
  • Model **nocturnal activity** linked to water temperature and rain.

5. Nesting and Reproduction Habits

Females emerge from the water only to nest. Nesting activity drives much of the increased movement observed in females during the late spring and early summer.

Reproductive Cycle

  • **Maturity:** Males mature earlier ($4 - 6$ years) than females ($6 - 8$ years).
  • **Nesting Season:** Peak seasonal activity occurs in late spring and early summer (April to July).
  • **Clutches:** Females typically lay **two clutches per year** (Ovulation in May and June).
  • **Clutch Size:** Very small, usually only **$1 - 3$ eggs** per clutch.
  • **Nest Location:** Nests are shallowly dug into the ground near the stream bank.
  • **Eggs:** Oblong with brittle shells, requiring $45$ to $122$ days to incubate, depending on temperature.
