Gopher Tortoise (Gopherus polyphemus)

Alabama's Keystone Species

Research for High School AI Tracking Model

1. Species Facts & Movement

  • Keystone Species: Their deep burrows provide critical shelter for around 360 other species.
  • Physical Size: Typically 9 to 15 inches long.
  • Lifespan: Can live 40-60 years in the wild.
  • Uniqueness: The only tortoise species found naturally east of the Mississippi River.
  • Status: Federally listed as Threatened west of the Mobile and Tombigbee Rivers in Alabama.

Locomotion & Burrowing

  • Adaptations: Uses flattened, shovel-like forelimbs for digging and sturdy, elephantine hind limbs for walking.
  • Time Spent: They are diurnal (active during the day) but spend up to 80% of their time inside their burrows to regulate temperature and humidity.
  • Burrow Size: Burrows average 15-20 feet long and 6.5 feet deep, but can reach 40 feet long and 10 feet deep.
  • Foraging Range: Typically forage for grasses, forbs, and fruits within 160 feet of their burrow entrance.

2. Habitat & Alabama Distribution

Ideal Habitat Requirements

Gopher tortoises require three key conditions, often met by the Longleaf Pine Ecosystem:

  • Soil: Well-drained, deep sandy soils for easy burrow excavation.
  • Canopy: Open tree canopy (sparse coverage) to allow sunlight for basking, nesting, and plant growth (for forage).
  • Management: Frequent prescribed fire is the single most important tool to maintain open canopy and ground cover.

Location in Alabama

Found primarily in the lower third of the state (Coastal Plains region).

  • Physiographic Region: Coastal Plains (below the Fall Line).
  • Key Counties: Found in over 20 counties, including Mobile, Baldwin, Conecuh, Escambia, and Houston.
  • Major Populations: Conecuh National Forest and Fort Rucker Military Base house the largest known populations.

3. Reproduction & Nesting Habits

  • Nesting Season: Late April to mid-July. Females lay only one clutch per year.
  • Clutch Size: Typically 5 to 9 eggs (up to 15). Eggs are about the size of a ping-pong ball.
  • Nest Location: Nests are frequently dug into the soft sand of the burrow apronâ€”the mound of loose sand right outside the entrance.
  • Incubation: Eggs hatch after 100-110 days, with hatchlings emerging from late August through November.
  • Sex Determination: Like some other reptiles, the sex of the hatchlings is determined by the temperature of the nest (Temperature-dependent Sex Determination).

The Burrow Apron (Key Sign)

The apron is the most visible sign of a tortoise. It's the fan-shaped deposit of sand excavated by the tortoise. The nesting female prefers this open, sunny spot, making the apron a key area for surveying and identifying nesting behavior.

4. Data for Tracking Models

Track & Footprint Analysis

Gopher tortoise tracks are distinctive due to their shovel-like front feet. The tracks can be used for modeling, particularly near burrows and foraging trails.

  • Track Shape: Front feet tracks are broad and fan-like; hind feet tracks are more elephantine and stubby.
  • Key Feature: Look for the distinctive drag mark from the plastron (bottom shell) or the shovel shape of the forelimbs in soft sand.
  • Model Input: Tracks, scat (feces), and the size/orientation of the burrow apron are common inputs for occupancy and density models.

Search Query for specific images: "Gopher Tortoise footprint track identification"

Modern Tracking Technology

AI/GIS models rely on data collected from high-tech sources:

  • GPS/Radio Telemetry: Small transmitters attached to the shell track real-time movement and home range, giving data on seasonal migration.
  • Remote Sensing (LiDAR): Used to map optimal habitat features like canopy height and soil type across large areas.
  • Citizen Science Apps: Public reporting (with photos and GPS) helps researchers track sightings and burrows for large-scale distribution mapping.
